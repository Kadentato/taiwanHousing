Taiwan Housing Explorer

About this project

← Back to the map

Short version: it's a map and a searchable table of house prices across Taiwan, built out of the government's open transaction data. This page is me explaining what it's for and how the thing actually works, because I think the how is the interesting part.

What it's actually for

Everything here comes from one source — Taiwan's 實價登錄 ("real price registration") data. By law, property deals have to be reported, and the Ministry of the Interior dumps all of it out for free: the price, the floor area, the number of rooms, the year the building went up, whether it came with a parking space. Genuinely open, genuinely public. Also genuinely a pain to open and understand, which is most of the reason this site exists.

What you get from the government is a folder of 200-odd CSV files, in Mandarin, split across several files you have to stitch back together. You can't just look at that and learn anything. So the goal of the site, in one sentence: take a data dump that's technically open but practically unreadable, and turn it into something you can click around in a browser without knowing any Chinese or any SQL. Pick a district, see what a place costs, watch how it's moved over the years, compare it to everywhere else, filter down to "apartments with parking in Kaohsiung," and grab the raw numbers if you want them.

The data fights back

A few things about the raw files ate way more of my time than I expected:

  • The dates use the ROC (Minguo) calendar. Year 115 is 2026 — you add 1911. Simple once you know it, except it's written as seven digits jammed together like 1150601, and some columns use 97年3月19日 instead, and rentals hand you a date range with a tilde in the middle. So the first thing I wrote was a parser that swallows all of those and spits out normal dates.
  • Floor counts are written in Chinese numerals. 十二層 means twelve floors. So there's a little function that turns 一/十/百 into integers, which is a weirdly satisfying thing to write.
  • Every file has two header rows — Chinese, then an auto-translated English one that's rough enough ("the berth category" for parking type) that I threw it out and did the translations myself.
  • The "main use" column has 239 distinct values. Not rows — values. Including typos and a catch-all that literally says "see the other registration items." You can't hardcode 239 cases, so I translate the common ones, bucket the long tail as "other," and keep the original text so nothing's lost.
  • The one nice surprise: every record has a serial number (編號) that links the main file to its land / building / parking sub-files, and it was unique everywhere with zero orphans. Government data is almost never that clean and I honestly didn't trust it until I'd checked it three times.

Making it a real database

All of it lands in a single SQLite file. I like SQLite here because it's one file, there's no server to run, and it's the same engine your phone uses under the hood. The schema follows how you'd actually think about the geography: a house sits in a district, a district in a city/county, a city in a region (north / central / south / east / islands). So the numbers roll up and down that hierarchy. There's also a tagging system — has parking, has a management committee, has an elevator — so you can slice by features instead of only by place.

What gets thrown away in cleaning (and what doesn't)

A fair question anyone should ask: if the site "cleans up" the data, what did it quietly delete? Here's the whole chain, with the actual counts, because the honest answer is "almost nothing you'd want":

  • Start: 4,825,329 raw sale rows across every quarterly file.
  • De-duplicate on the serial number — the same deal shows up in overlapping releases — drops 15,053 (0.3%), leaving 4,810,276.
  • Drop land-only and parking-only transactions (a bare plot or a lone parking space isn't a home) — that's the one big cut, 1,316,454 rows (27%), leaving 3,493,822 housing sales.
  • Date sanity — impossible or missing dates — removes about 9,000 (0.2%), leaving 3,484,620.
  • "The numbers don't add up" — 71,877 rows (2.06%). This is the one worth explaining. A reviewer noticed records like an 88 m² home with 5 bedrooms and 4 bathrooms — which can't be a real single home. Auditing every sale turned up a small tail where the government's raw fields contradict each other, so a row is dropped if the price is a clear typo (outside NT$5k–3M/m²), the room counts are nonsense (>12 bedrooms or >10 bathrooms), the floor area can't physically hold the layout, or the rooms are impossibly dense — four or more rooms averaging under 10 m² each. That last rule is what catches the 88 m² case (about 8 m² per room), while normal small apartments (13–15 m²/room) sail through untouched. These are almost always townhouses whose recorded transfer area covers a single floor while the layout field counts the whole building; we can't repair them per-record, so we drop them.

So cleaning removes one deliberate slice (land/parking, 27%) and then ~2% of genuine data-quality junk. A city's month-by-month sale volume comes through basically intact, and the map's medians barely move — but the raw records you can click into and download are now free of the impossible ones.

And to be clear about what you're looking at: nothing here is sampled. The map's colours and the "current selection" numbers come from pre-computed full-data medians and counts, so a city's transaction count is the real total, not an estimate. The time chart is the complete monthly series. And when you click into a district, the site fetches that district's entire set of sales — every single one, even the big districts with 100,000+ — and draws them all, then computes the median, spread and confidence interval from the lot. It stays fast because it only ever loads the one district you asked for, compressed, on demand — not the whole country up front.

One more honest wrinkle: the newest months always look thin, because the government discloses deals in batches with a lag. The site treats any month that's clearly still filling in (under half the usual volume) as "not final yet" so it doesn't mislead the trend.

The map, and the annoying fact that there are no coordinates

Here's what the data flatly refuses to give you: latitude and longitude. You get a Chinese address and a district name, and that's it. So to put anything on a map I had to bring my own geography. I pulled an open dataset of Taiwan's township boundaries and worked out a centre point for each district.

This is where it gets a bit maths-y, and it's my favourite part. You can't just average lat/long to find a centre, because degrees aren't a flat, even grid — a degree of longitude is a different real distance depending on how far north you are. So I reproject the shapes into EPSG:3826, a coordinate system built specifically for Taiwan where the units are actual metres, find the centroid there, then convert back to lat/long for the map. Same trick any time I'd want a real distance later (like "how far to the nearest school" — not in yet, but the schema has a slot waiting for it).

For the coloured regions, I take all the little township shapes and dissolve them into one city or region blob. All the geometry work is geopandas and Shapely, which is the standard Python stack for this, and I store the shapes as WKT text right in the database so they load straight back into geopandas — or open in QGIS — with no conversion.

Honest caveat: since I only have district centres, every house in a district gets dropped on the exact same point. So the map is great for "where are the expensive clusters" and useless for "find me this one building." That's a deliberate trade. Real per-house coordinates would mean geocoding tens of thousands of addresses, which is slow and rate-limited, so I left hooks in the database for it and moved on.

Why it's just files, with no server

The whole front end is plain HTML, CSS, and JavaScript. No React, no framework, because it's a map and two charts, not air traffic control. The map is Leaflet, the time chart is Chart.js, both loaded from a CDN. There's no backend at all — Python builds the database and then exports everything the page needs as static JSON and GeoJSON, and the browser does the rest.

The part I like most is that the filtering happens live in the browser. Tick "has parking" or flip to rentals and it recomputes the medians on the spot from the raw records. I use the median instead of the mean on purpose: there's a single transaction in here worth something like NT$1.7 billion, and one number like that drags an average all over the place — the median just shrugs.

The payoff of building it this way is that the entire site is a folder of files. Which means it can be hosted free, basically forever, on something like GitHub Pages or Netlify, with nothing to keep running and nothing to pay for. For a student project that might just sit online, that matters a lot.

Stuff I already know is imperfect

  • Outside the five biggest metros (Taipei, New Taipei, Taichung, Taoyuan, Tainan, which are now geocoded to real street addresses), houses still land on their district's centre point — great for "where are the expensive clusters," useless for "find me this one building."
  • School distance is stubbed out in the schema but not actually filled in.
  • Big districts (100k+ sales) draw every dot on one canvas, so the very first click into one can take a few seconds to fetch and render; after that it's instant. Colours and the time chart use the full data.
  • The raw table will cheerfully show you the outliers, because it's meant to be the honest, unfiltered view. The map hides them behind medians; the table doesn't.

Why I bothered

None of this is groundbreaking. It's mostly the unglamorous work of taking data that exists but is annoying and making it not annoying. But that turns out to be a huge chunk of what data work actually is, and I picked up a pile doing it — map projections, why medians beat means, how to lay out a schema you won't hate in a month. If you clicked around the map and noticed something you didn't know about Taiwan, that was the whole idea.
